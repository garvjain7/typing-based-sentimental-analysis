From the first wheel to the first microchip, every invention has carried with it the weight of expectation and the shadow of unintended consequence. Today we carry devices in our pockets that contain more computing power than entire buildings once held. The ocean at night is a different creature entirely from the one you see during the day. The water turns black and infinite, erasing the horizon so completely that sky and sea become one seamless darkness. Waves that seemed playful in daylight take on a heavier sound, a deeper rhythm, as if the ocean is breathing slowly in its sleep. Standing at the edge of it in the dark, you feel the full weight of your smallness in a way that is not entirely unpleasant. There is something clarifying about confronting something so vast and so indifferent. It does not know your name. It does not care about your problems. It simply continues, as it has for millions of years, rolling and receding and rolling again.

Her colleagues occasionally asked, with varying degrees of sincerity, whether she ever wished she had chosen something more dramatic to dedicate her life to. She always gave the same answer. Every organism is a solution to a problem, she would say. Every creature alive today is the result of an unbroken chain of survival stretching back to the beginning of life on this planet. There is nothing small about that. The beetle she studied had developed a mechanism for harvesting water from fog that engineers were now trying to replicate for use in drought-stricken regions.
